Production cuts in Venezuela not going over well with workers

  • January 7th, 2010 11:50 am ET

Venalum Logo (Gob de Venezuela)

In the Venezuelan government’s latest attempt to save electricity during drought-induced power rationing, it has ordered production cuts at mining companies in southern Guayana until the country's main hydroelectric plant can resume normal output.

However, according to El Universal, aluminum smelter workers at the state-run Venalum company are anything but happy with the austerity measures.

A group of workers and trade union leaders held a protest to reject the energy rationing measures, which involves the temporary shutdown of 360 electrolytic cells. That will mean a reduction of around 14,000 tons in monthly output.

Workings are refusing to allow the closure of a single electrolytic cell, saying that whenever an electrolytic cell is closed, at least five years are needed to reopen it.

"These measures don't fix the electricity problem, but it will affect the lives of thousands of workers who stand to lose their jobs. It will have a serious impact on the area economy," union leader Manuel Díaz told AFP. "They didn't consult the Venezuelan people about shutting down production, and even less the workers."

According to the Laredo Sun, Electricity Minister Angel Rodriguez on Monday told El Mundo daily that the government may close down its nationalized aluminum and steel industries altogether in its battle to save energy.

"If we have to close the industries in Guayana because Guri (reservoir) has dried out, we'll have to do it," the minister said.

Venezuela’s electrical grid has been suffering from too much demand and in inability to meet that demand due to aging infrastructure. In order to update that grid, the Venezuelan government is taking out an $800 million loan from the Inter-American Development Bank.

 

The recent drought—the most severe in 45 years—is also affecting neighboring countries like Ecuador, which is also rationing water and electricity.
