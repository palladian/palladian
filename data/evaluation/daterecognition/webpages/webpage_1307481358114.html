Brown looks at US-style home loans

By Ben Leapman, Evening Standard Last updated at 18:51pm on 09.05.03

 Add your view

 

THE GOVERNMENT is to examine plans to introduce 30-year fixed-rate mortgages in an attempt to stabilise Britain's volatile housing market. The American-style deals would protect property buyers from any rise in interest rates. If rates fell homeowners could switch without paying a penalty.

Chancellor Gordon Brown's adviser on the home loan market, Professor David Miles, believes the new-look mortgages could reduce risk for property buyers. They could be proposed in a report on the mortgage market which he is drafting at the Chancellor's request.

Britain is unique in having a high proportion of householders on variable-rate mortgages, vulnerable to any rise in interest rates. In the early Nineties, a sharp rise in the base rate under the Tory Government helped to plunge thousands of householders into negative equity, fuelling a recession. The unusual structure of the mortgage market in Britain is one of the key factors making our economy different from those in continental Europe.

Changing the way Britons borrow to buy homes could make it easier for the Government to demonstrate that the British and continental economies are converging - one of the key tests which must be passed before Britain can join the euro.

The key question for the Government will be how banks and building societies could be encouraged to offer the deals. One or two have provided long-term fixed-rate deals in the past but they have proved unpopular with customers, largely because of the large penalties imposed for switching out mid-way through the term.

Supporters of the British system point out that there is a free market in mortgages, with tough competition between rival lenders to offer the most attractive and competitive deals.

Professor Miles said he would examine whether there were any obstacles preventing lenders from providing American-style mortgages.

The professor will try to establish whether British property buyers are happy with the kind of deals currently on offer, or whether they want a more secure option.

He said: 'Maybe they just like living dangerously, but that is rather odd since they don't appear to have that approach to other areas of their life. British people tend to be risk-averse.'

The Bank of England's success in keeping inflation under control has reduced fears among the house-buying public that interest rates will ever spiral out of control. Ministers regularly trumpet Labour's success in keeping interest rates down.
