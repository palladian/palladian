State Cuts Could Impact Court System

Posted: 10:55 pm EDT March 28, 2011Updated: 5:57 am EDT March 29, 2011

State funding cuts could have a crippling effect on the North Carolina courts system, said court administrators on Monday.

In an email sent to court workers and judges, proposed cuts to Judicial and Public Safety Appropriations include laying off more than 700 workers, reducing the technology budget by $10.2 million, and eliminating Drug Treatment and Family courts. All told, Gov. Bev Perdue has asked JPS and all state agencies to trim their budgets by 10 percent.

�We�re a lean operation already,� said Charles Keller, the Mecklenburg County Court Outreach Administrator. �It would certainly cripple us in some areas and cut to the bare bones in other areas.�

Channel 9 took the potential cuts to Mecklenburg County District Attorney Andrew Murray, who had not yet seen the list of suggestions. He said the public would ultimately pay the price.

�We�re not going to be able to do the job,� he said. �We�re not going to be able to keep the public safe the way that they�re accustomed to.�

Eliminating the Drug Treatment and Family courts have been proposed before, but those in the legal community told Channel 9 that the cuts appear to be much likelier this year. Without Drug such courts, cases would have to go through traditional court instead.

�It�s really counterproductive to take away some of these programs,� Murray said. �It�s cutting of your nose to spite your face.�

He pointed to Drug Treatment Court as an example. Data from administrators showed the likelihood of repeat drug offenses without the court program is quadrupled. Data also showed processing those same offenders through traditional court would cost the state nearly $5 million more.

�We�re looking at eliminating a lot of programs that save us a lot of time and money,� Keller said. �It�s a scary thought that it could actually become a reality this year.�

Even if all the proposed cuts were enacted, JPS would still be roughly $50 million short of its goal. Those in the legal community said it would be hard to imagine where else the system could endure additional cuts. That�s why Murray was planning to travel to Raleigh on Wednesday, hoping to convince state lawmakers otherwise.
