Who will benefit from Arts Council England's Sustain fund?

A pot of £40m has been made available to arts organisations hit by the recession, but transparency will be highly important when the cash is handed out

    • Tweet this
  • Comments (…)

I'm a bit late off the starting blocks with this one: you're probably all aware that in the same week that Arts Council England saw an immediate £4m cut in its budget, it announced a new £40m open fund for arts organisations suffering because of the economic downturn. With applications open from 1 June, the Sustain fund will be available over the next two years (or until the money is spent) and give grants between £75,000 and £3m to arts organisations hit by the recession. It aims to offer a rapid response with no more than six weeks between application and decision. The money has become available because of a substantial reduction in Lottery cash balances held by the Arts Council, which is all well and good – just so long as lottery sales hold up.

Now, £40m is a pretty hefty whack of money (think about what happened with the £25m from the theatre review back in 2001), and I'm not one to look a gift-horse in the mouth, but I will be intrigued to see exactly how this fund operates and exactly who benefits. The size of the grants and the fact that individual artists are excluded (they are being directed towards Grants for the Arts, which sees a rise of £2m to £54m – also taken from Lottery cash balances) rather suggests that it is the big buildings that are most likely to benefit. Another big question is how much flexibility there is in the criteria. Will a really deserving case such as the Brewhouse in Taunton be eligible? (It could be argued that its difficulties predate the recession.) And exactly how is ACE going to decide whether those applying have really been hit by the recession or just have an eye to the main chance?

Those with long memories may recall the stabilisation fund of the late 1990s, which put millions of pounds and teams of management consultants into theatres that continued to fail, albeit more expensively. This scheme will have to be extremely transparent in its decision-making to avoid any suspicion that this is a backdoor way of getting money to organisations that are already ailing, but which ACE feels can't be allowed to fail for political reasons.

My preference would have been for a far greater chunk of the money to go into the already pressured Grants for the Arts pot, which supports the theatre ecology from the bottom up rather than the top down, funding work directly rather than supporting bureaucracies. But we shall see. I very much hope that when the first funds are awarded I will be eating my words.

Posted by Lyn Gardner Tuesday 5 May 2009 12.10 BST guardian.co.uk

    I've read this, and the thread about the Brewhouse theatre in Taunton. Spare a moment to consider:

    In the year ending '08, there were 23 regularly funded organisations devoted to theatre in the south west, compared to only 3 RFO galleries in the entire south west region.     The key phrase in the the Sustain fund is:

  • RobertMiles

    8 May 2009 11:22AM

    This fund was announced just as we at The Brewhouse were urgently talking to our stakeholders about the sustainability of our innovative approach to how we serve our community. We embrace change and as a medium sized arts organisation feel it is our responsibility to be fleet of foot and pilot new models which can be used by others. All of this alongside our serious financial problems should put us in a strong position to be helped by this fund.

    The issue, as you point out Lyn, is how we prove that our problems are a result of the current financial climate. Long term underfunding has created our problems, but the recession has accelerated the outcome and led to our current crisis. We will be putting forward an application, but are well aware of the competition, many of whom will have far more sophisticated lobbying systems than a small but ambitious arts centre in Somerset.

    I read the news of this fund with a buzz of excitement as it seemed to be exactly what we need, just as we need it. The Brewhouse will be a test case to see if this money can do what it says on the tin, and if the regions are still considered an important part of the arts ecology.

    Robert Miles, Director, The Brewhouse
