Switching on his talent

Posted by RedStrat in News Updates | 1 comment

Apr 12, 2010

By Ben Ezeamalu

Otejiri Oghoghorie is three things rolled into one – a student, marketer, and an inventor. A casual glance across his room at Abule Egba, Lagos even proves this.

A mishmash of tiny electrical and electronic parts, his working tools, and a handful of text books were spread across his reading cum working table, and a huge back pack with which he conveys the automatic changeover, a device which he invented, was propped at a corner of the room.

Solving a problem

To ease the inconvenience usually associated with switching off generators and switching over to the national power supply whenever it is restored, Mr. Oghoghorie said he was motivated to invent the device.

“I invented the changeover due to the unstable power supply, and before I invented it,” the 23 year old said. “I realised that most portable generator users do not know when NEPA (the power company) is on and so their generator keeps running until someone comes to tell them to switch it off.”

According to him, the device, once installed, will automatically switch from generator power (when it is on) and at the same time switch off the generating set. He estimates the speed of changeover to be 0.1 seconds because, according to him, it is so fast that the switching goes unnoticed.

Defining moment

Like every novel idea, the creation of the automatic changeover came with its own headaches.

“The first day I started working on it, I had my first meal by 6 pm. There was nobody to go to. It was just me, my God, and the internet. But I knew I was going somewhere,” the 200 level student recalls. “Also, I did not have friends to share the idea with. The friends I had were people who just wanted to go to school and work in a white man’s company”

Mr. Oghoghorie said his dream was to do something that would get him the recognition of the Vice Chancellor of Onabisi Onabanjo University, where he is studying Physics and Electronics, but when recognition finally came, he was unprepared for it: his nomination as one of the three finalists in the Best Use of Science Category for the 2009 The Future Awards held at the Muson Centre, Lagos.

Though he did not win, he said it was the proudest moment of his life.

“I attended with my dad. My dad is an average educated man and so I was happy we were able to sit with dignitaries and kings. It was a very proud moment.”

Mr. Oghoghorie has never played down the influence of his father in his aspiration. “My dad always tells me that whenever you are loosing a screw; write it down so that you can easily replace it. He has really guided me in this,” he said.

Studying and inventing

Between studying at school in Ogun State and handling Runetech, the technical division of Ruona Ufuoma Nigeria Enterprises, his father’s company in Lagos; Mr. Oghoghorie said he still finds time to work on more inventions. A battery charger that does not require electricity and a pest repellent that utilises sound waves to drive away pests are among the things he is currently working on.

“I do my research on the internet and textbooks. And my experience in Electrical Electronics at The Polytechnic, Ife, where I had a National Diploma has also been useful,” he says.

Asides the financial hiccups which he describes as a â€˜major setback,’ Mr. Oghoghorie says he is also facing the problem of competent hands to help with the marketing and installation of the changeover device.

“I am training seven people to help me with this and also to enable them stand on their own,” he said.

One person who is already reaping the dividend of the one year old automatic changeover device is Ekene Nwosu, a hotelier, who says he has used it for more than six months.

“My reason for installing it is that sometimes when it is very late, I’ll be scared to go out,” he said, showing the device fastened to the wall in his room. “I’ll allow the fuel to finish so that it will go off, and it is not good for the generator’s engine. Now, if I want to off the generator, even if it is 2 am, I’ll just go there and press it and it will go off.”

Moving forward

Also on Mr. Oghoghorie’s table were a computer desktop and laptop. He said he uses the internet mainly to update his knowledge in the field of electronics.

“I’ve never regretted it. I’d choose electronics over food. When I’m working, the whole world is within the room,” he said.
