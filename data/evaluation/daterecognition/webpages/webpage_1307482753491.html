By Valerie Cox

Friday May 03 2002

AT DAWN yesterday, 700 soldiers came out of camouflage having successfully infiltrated enemy positions and taken dozens of 'prisoners of war'.

The Irish battalion had heard rumours of a mass grave but, as they were investigating this, five were 'killed' by the enemy.

It was the Army's biggest exercise in decades in the Glen of Imaal in Co Wicklow - training in combined operations for the EU Rapid Reaction Force where Ireland has promised a light infantry battalion.

The exercise was the end of a fictional civil war and the men were facing a "warlord type scenario" with unprovoked attacks by the rebels, played by the FCA in their new camouflage uniforms.

Capt Rory Finnegan stressed this was a real outing for the soldiers, who had to spend five days and five nights in the mountains, living on Army rations and without access to proper washing facilities.

Most of the soldiers had already notched up service in the Lebanon and East Timor. Another 200 will leave for Eritrea at the end of May and, by 2003, the Army has pledged to have a light infantry battalion ready and operational for peacekeeping duties overseas.

The officer commanding the exercise, Lt Col David Prendergast described the treacherous conditions underfoot as the soldiers tramped from Glenmalure over Table Mountain in the darkness.

There were a number of real injuries en-route - three men who fell had to be taken to hospital by ambulance and another eight were confined to the camp's sick bay with more minor injuries.

Lt Col Prendergast said it was a very important exercise because it prepared the soldiers for their peacekeeping duties overseas.

The troops, heavily armed with Steyr rifles, Mag machine guns and backed up by Scorpion tanks and Milan guided missiles, were deployed throughout the wet countryside.

A bird's eye view by helicopter showed hundreds of troops arriving cold, wet and hungry, as they dried out their "bashas", or shelters, under which they had tried to catch a few hours sleep. Then they tucked in to Army rations of pork sausages and beans and Irish stew.

- Valerie Cox
