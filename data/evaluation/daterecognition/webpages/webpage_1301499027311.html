Abele pays no state income tax

  • e-mail
  • print

Feb. 7, 2011 |(136) Comments

Click to enlarge
Chris Abele
Jeff Stone
Lee Holloway
Jim Sullivan
Election 2011

Complete coverage of the spring races for state, county, municipal and judicial offices.

  • Primary results
  • Election section

Chris Abele, the millionaire running for Milwaukee County executive, has made a name for himself over the past 15 years by giving away wads of cash to arts organizations, do-gooder groups and Democratic politicians.

But the Milwaukee philanthropist has shown himself to be tightfisted with one particular entity:

The State of Wisconsin.

For at least the past four years, Abele has paid no state income taxes.

Not one nickel.

It's a stunning disclosure about the only county exec candidate who can afford to dip into his wallet to fill the local airwaves with campaign ads before the Feb. 15 primary.

"Chris Abele has paid the taxes he's owed," said Abele campaign aide Brandon Lorenz, who confirmed that the candidate also had no federal tax liability last year.

Lorenz noted that Abele, the son of a co-founder of the medical equipment company Boston Scientific, draws no regular salary from his family foundation, makes significant personal donations to charity and pays a five-figure property tax bill.

That's all well and good, but people won't like hearing that Abele can ignore paying Uncle Sam on April 15, said Andrew Reschovsky, a University of Wisconsin-Madison economics professor. That's because, he said, most Americans expect those with more resources to pay more in taxes.

Abele's example "doesn't mean there's fraud going on. There's no reason to believe anyone is cheating on their taxes," Reschovsky said. "It's just the nature of our income tax system, and that's why more and more people think that we need fundamental tax reform."

While not as surprising as the Abele numbers, records show that two other county executive candidates are turning over relatively small sums to the state, despite drawing government paychecks.

In 2009, state Rep. Jeff Stone, a Greendale Republican, paid out about $1,600 in state income taxes and County Board Chairman Lee Holloway only $1,335.

Those sums would suggest that each official reported taxable income between $20,000 and $25,000 last year. State residents can reduce their tax burden through a variety of means, such as making large charitable donations or suffering business losses.

The state, meanwhile, has no record that another candidate, community activist Ieshuh Griffin, has paid any state income tax in recent years.

Indeed, only one of the five county exec contenders is making a significant payment to Wisconsin tax collectors year after year.

Former state Sen. Jim Sullivan, a Wauwatosa Democrat, plunked down $5,266 in state income taxes last year - more than the other four candidates combined. In addition to drawing his state salary, Sullivan is also a private attorney.

"Like most Milwaukee residents, the Sullivan family works hard and pays their fair share," said Sullivan campaign aide Gillian Morris.

Morris declined to discuss the amounts paid by the other candidates, saying she would leave that to the voters.

Holloway, however, jumped at the chance to attack Abele, the billionaire's son. At first, Holloway said, he was shocked to find out that Abele didn't have to send tax money to Madison like everybody else.

But Holloway said it does make sense. Abele, he said, doesn't have a regular job that pays a salary from which taxes must be taken.

"I've always said he had a silver spoon in his mouth," Holloway said. "This proves it."

As for his own skimpy payments, he pointed to his now-infamous 15 rental properties.

Holloway is facing six Municipal Court lawsuits over the properties and more than 100 building code violations. Police have reported calls at his properties 863 times over the past two years.

In an interview, the north side politician said he has been losing money on his buildings for the past eight years and been unsuccessfully trying to sell them for the past five. He earned more than $71,000 as the County Board boss in '09.

Stone offers a similar explanation.

At one point, he said, he employed as many as eight people at his printing shop. But Stone, who earns slightly less than $50,000 a year as a state lawmaker, said he has significantly scaled back the business to the point that only he and his wife are putting time into it.

He said he is now focusing more attention on his legislative work and taking a hit to his bottom line as a result.

"I make less money now than I did 10 years ago," Stone said.

Yeah, not a particularly compelling campaign bumper sticker, even if true.

Griffin didn't return calls about her taxes on Monday and has a voicemail message in which she said she is no longer talking to the mainstream media. Griffin, whose legal name is Inez, argued last week that it was illegal to request her state income taxes. She even threatened to sue if the Journal Sentinel followed through with a request for her records.

As for Abele, his campaign staffer emphasized that the first-time candidate paid more than $64,000 in property taxes last year. Abele bought his house on N. Lake Drive for $2.6 million in 2005.

On his personal financial disclosure statement, Abele said he owned at least $5,000 worth of 100 different stocks or partnerships, held at least a 10% ownership of two corporations and had a stake in five other companies. He also reported 36 debts or obligations and said he owed more than $50,000 to the 2003 trust created by his parents for Abele and his siblings.

For about a decade, Abele has run his family's foundation, called the Argosy Foundation. The group's tax records show Abele was paid only $1,393 in salary and nearly $14,000 in deferred compensation and employee benefits last year. The foundation was built using Abele's father's wealth.

Abele's father, John, fell from Forbes' list of billionaires last year because of a plunge in his company's stock price. Having traded at more than $45 per share in 2004, Boston Scientific stock is now below $7.

So, in general, how does someone like Abele avoid paying any income taxes?

Andrew Komisar, a Milwaukee tax attorney, said there are several possibilities.

Without talking about Abele's case specifically, Komisar said it's possible someone in his situation suffered significant losses and spread out the sums over several years. That would significantly offset any income.

In addition, he said, a wealthy individual can make enough in charitable donations to reduce the amount owed in state taxes.

Lorenz, Abele's spokesman, said the philanthropist gave away $38,222 to 12 Milwaukee-area groups last year, compared with $94,740 a year earlier and $18,582 in 2008. Those donations came in addition to the money dispersed by Argosy.

Finally, Komisar said, an individual could receive distributions from a trust and pay no income taxes if the money came from the trust's principal, not its earnings.

Another tax lawyer, Joel Joyce, didn't dispute any of those possibilities. But Joyce said it's important not to read too much into the amounts people pay or don't pay in state income taxes.

"This information that the state is required to give you," he said, "is of very little help in determining what people's income is."
