Battered Rangers Ponder Cliff Lee on Short Rest, Woeful Bullpen

Oct 29, 2010 – 8:05 PM
Text Size
Email This

John Hickey %BloggerTitle%

ARLINGTON, Texas -- There is plenty of precedent for teams coming back to win the World Series after having lost the first two games.

It's been done 11 times in 51 tries, or about 20 percent of the time.

What is unprecedented is the way a San Francisco offense that is arguably the weakest of any World Series team in at least a decade has beaten up a Texas pitching staff that might well be the best in team history.

The Rangers have been mauled for 20 runs in the first two games, much of that blood being on the hands of the bullpen, which has an 18.68 ERA.

So what does Texas manager Ron Washington do heading into the Rangers' first home game of the series Saturday in Rangers Ballpark?

He could move up Cliff Lee, his ace, to pitch on three days' rest in Game 4 Sunday and skip Tommy Hunter, his fourth starter.

He could go to closer Neftali Feliz earlier, rather than not at all -- as was the case Thursday in Game 2 when a two-out, seven-run inning against four Texas pitchers might have been cut short by an appearance from the closer.

Asked about Lee Friday, Washington was evasive.

"The only thing I'm thinking about is Colby (Lewis, the Game 3 starter) tomorrow," Washington said. "I can't answer that."

Lee, who was a perfect 7-0 in eight postseason starts before getting beat up in Game 1 by the Giants in an 11-7 loss, has never thrown on three days' rest. Some pitchers adapt to it well, others don't.

With no history to use as a guide, Washington will be throwing the dice if he goes with Lee over Hunter.

In the American League Championship Series, the Yankees went with their ace, CC Sabathia, on three days' rest, and his performance was a vast improvement on his Game 1 performance.

Looking at it historically, Elias Sports Bureau says that from 2007-2009, nine postseason games were started on short rest. Those starts produced three wins and four losses and an ERA well in excess of 6.00.

Still, that was then and this is now, as they say.

Other that being friends, former Indians, former Cy Young Award winners and left-handers, however, Lee and Sabathia don't have much in common. Sabathia is a power pitcher. Lee is a finesse pitcher. Both can dip into the other's strength as needed, but their success comes from staying with their own strengths.

The Texas clubhouse was closed after Friday's workout, so what, exactly, Lee thinks about the possibility of starting on short rest will have to wait. Asked earlier in the season, however, he indicated he was at least open to the idea.

But when you ask Lee the keys to his success the last few years, the answer is invariably the same.

"Pitching with confidence and relying on my routine," Lee said. "Going out there and expecting to be successful."

Starting Game 4 on short rest would mean, among other things, that Lee would be out of his routine, and you'd have to think that would run afoul of his confidence. Can the Rangers afford that?

If they win Saturday and are down 2-1 in the World Series, maybe not. If they lose Saturday and are down 3-0, they may have no option. When the Rangers were up 2-1 in the AL Division Series against Tampa Bay, Lee said no one asked him then if he'd throw on short rest.

Lee did pitch in Game 5 and justified the Rangers' confidence, dominating the Rays in St. Petersburg for the clinching win.



As for the bullpen, confidence would seem to be in short supply. Check out these World Series ERAs: Derek Holland, infinite (three earned runs, zero outs recorded); Michael Kirkman, 13.50; Mark Lowe, infinite (two earned runs, zero outs recorded) and Darren O'Day, 18.00. Alexi Ogando and Darren Oliver have each thrown an inning without giving up any runs. And Feliz hasn't pitched.

This is a group that came together over the course of the season and wound up being a major factor in the Texas success story. The cumulative ERA of the Texas bullpen was the second best in the league.

Against the Giants, however, the welling up of confidence was lost.

"It's not a good feeling," catcher Matt Treanor said in looking back at the Giants' seven-run eighth inning, "every time you see someone put the ball in play hard or you walk a guy with the bases loaded."

Holland, who started the Thursday meltdown by throwing balls with his first 11 pitches and walking in two runs, doesn't shy away from the uphill climb facing the Rangers.

"We're an underdog now," he said. "(But) that's what we've been all year. We're not going to back down from that. We're going to come back."

There's no time like the present.
