Injuries have hampered Weeks throughout career

Phoenix — The fact that hitters despise shadows is no secret.

It's a common and tiresome complaint, especially during day games at Miller Park.

And one day early last season, Rickie Weeks got fed up with the gripes.

As the Milwaukee Brewer players occupied their dugout, some whined about the rays of sun coming through the window panes and how difficult it makes recognizing pitches.

That's when Weeks, normally a soft-spoken, almost muted individual, decided to speak his mind.

"Shut up about the shadows," Weeks surprisingly exclaimed. "They ain't going nowhere. Deal with it."

The moaning stopped, and another example of Weeks' strong personality was exemplified.

"You have to be one of his teammates to really understand," Brewers manager Ken Macha said about his second baseman. "The typical fan says, 'Oh, his defense isn't too good. He swings at too many balls out of the strike zone.' But he brings the total package with his intensity and his work ethic and focus.

"There's no fooling around when the game starts with Rickie."

Unfortunately for Weeks, his career has sputtered since he was selected as the second-overall pick in the 2003 draft.

Injuries and quick peeks at his seemingly limitless potential have defined the 27-year-old's career to this point.

Since coming up as a full-time major league player in 2006, Weeks has not played an entire season because of three wrist injuries and a sprained knee.

And when he has been healthy, there have been only glimpses of the player the organization believed he would become when they drafted him as Baseball America magazine's College Player of the Year and Golden Spikes Award winner after his junior year at Southern University.

Because Weeks has not played a full season yet, he has been highly criticized by fans. At one point in 2008, there was even an ongoing cry for him to be traded.

Another disappointing end

But nothing was as disappointing as last season's left wrist injury, the same kind of tendon injury that limited him to 95 games in 2006, although that injury was to his right wrist.

Weeks had never burst into a season the way he did in 2009, driving in 24 runs and hitting nine home runs in his first 36 games. He was the catalyst at the top of the order, setting the table for Ryan Braun and Prince Fielder, while showing he has some punch of his own.

Even his defense, which had long been a glaring deficiency, was improving.

Then, he injured the wrist on a swing in St. Louis on May 17, and he never played another game last season.

Not being on the field hurt as much as all the injuries combined.

"The biggest thing was not being around the guys for so long and not being able to help out and watching the team play," Weeks said.

"When I got put in the cast they asked if I wanted to go home and I said, 'Definitely.' I just didn't want to be sitting there watching the game and not being able to do anything. When I did come back, I'd sit there thinking I could be doing something out there."

With all the expectations on his broad shoulders - plus the injuries and the statistics that many believed would be much more impressive at this point - Weeks has never wavered in his confidence and has never doubted himself.

He also hasn't let the frustrations and disappointments eat away at his mental strength, which, according to some people around him, is the reason he is still in the major leagues.

"The hard times he's gone through, it never affects him," said hitting coach Dale Sveum, who has been with Weeks through his entire major-league career. "The one thing about Rickie, he can make an error or two and it doesn't affect him. He's not thinking he's going to make another one or if he struck out three times, he doesn't care. He's able to forget about it.

"That's very difficult. That separates the big leaguers. Other people can't handle the negative things that happen in this game."

Weeks hates talking about the injuries and deflects any assertion his career has been slow to blossom. He doesn't even use the injuries or detractors as fuel or motivation.

Leading man

Weeks speaks with sincerity and delivers his thoughts in an intense yet low-key demeanor. When he speaks, people listen.

Just like they did in the dugout that day.

"That was beautiful," Macha said. "We missed that (leadership)."

The Brewers missed a lot about Weeks. Even though the pitching staff collapsed last season, Macha said losing his leadoff hitter for the season - the team was 10 games above .500 at the time of the injury and played 12 games below after - was a key turning point in the season.

The Brewers missed stoic presence and no-nonsense approach.

"Injuries are one thing, but being a No. 1 pick, the high expectations, not meeting them, receiving some of the wrath from fans because of (it)," Macha said. "You have to be mentally strong (to deal with that)."

For a player with a career.247 batting average and competing in about 95 games a season, it's uncommon to hear such high praise.

But everyone from the front office to his teammates to his coaches raves about Weeks as a player and person.

"When he's gone, you're losing a winning player," Sveum said. "As far as the intensity every day for nine innings, every pitch, a guy that cares about winning a baseball game, that is a trait that is very hard to find.

"And that is the ultimate compliment."

People often say there is a fine line between cocky and confident. While Weeks is far too quiet to be cocky, his confidence is undeniable, no matter how the first chapters of his career have been written.

When asked if he thinks he will finally have the kind of season to match his talent, Weeks told it raw.

"I will," Weeks said. "It's just a matter of coming in and doing my job, and I will do that. That's all I can say right now."
