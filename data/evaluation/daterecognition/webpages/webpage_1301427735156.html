Irving Penn: Your reviews

Read the winning reviews from our writing competition.

At the end of January, Telephoto invited readers to review the Irving Penn retrospective at the National Portrait Gallery. We selected 40 applicants, of which twenty have been chosen as finalists, published below.

The three overall winners, chosen by Sandy Nairn, Director of the National Portrait Gallery and Magda Kearny, Curator of the exhibition are Jane Whitfield, Surahbi S Khanna and Lauren Rotenberg. Special mention goes to Josephine Thum. All four will receive a copy of the artbook accompanying the show and a poster worth £20.

Finalist number twenty is Margaret Stewart

‘Sensitive people faced with the prospect of a camera portrait put on a face they think is the one they would like to show the world…Very often what lies behind the façade is rare and more wonderful than the subject knows or dares to believe.’

These words by Irving Penn at the beginning of the exhibition set the scene for some gritty and candid images. The façade is gone, we see emotion; confidence, joy, uncertainty, sadness, determination. The characters shine through; his focus on the eyes makes you believe that you can feel empathy with the sitter.

The exhibition starts with an impromptu picture of Giorgio de Chirico and spans seven decades including images from Vogue in the ‘40’s to just before his death. He uses very few props, early images just a corner or carpet thrown over boxes or chairs. The atmosphere austere and slightly scruffy prompted different reactions, The Duchess of Windsor cool and confident, Georgia O’Keeffe diminishing and uncomfortable. Much of the mood created with clever use of light and shadow, his preference being natural or simulated daylight.

His focus turns more to faces than full length images in the 50’s, examples being Richard Burton and Pablo Picasso not forgetting the stunning beauty of Audrey Hepburn. A brave ‘portrait’ of Saul Steinberg wearing a mask is amongst the 60’s selection. Another approach emerged in the 70’s and 80’s with some sitters like Arthur Miller being photographed with their eyes closed, Issey Miyake in deep shadow and Woody Allen portraying Charlie Chaplin. The images in this exhibition are timeless and the subjects entrancing.

Finalist number nineteen is Rebecca Ley

Spanning his entire career, what is most apparent from the exhibition of Irving Penn’s portraits at the National Portrait Gallery is his unwavering quality and style. Each portrait is shot in black and white, utterly truthful but utterly flattering. With every sitter he manages to coax out their character through the way they interact with the space he has given them in his studio.

Penn allows the subject’s nature to show by accentuating an expression or pose; the neutrality of the bare setting leaving the subject nowhere to hide. The result is for some, awkward, but for others it is empowering and only serves to prove self-assuredness. With Penn’s unique style, he strips away all the fuss that photographing well known figures tends to elicit; what we are left with is only the simplistic but dynamic method in which Penn exposes the individual nuances of each subject. He so artfully captures Grace Kelly’s beauty, Edith Piaf’s frailty, Picasso’s timidity.

The exhibition seems to be a testament to the character of his work; from the 1940s until his last work in 2007, he remains steadfast in his approach, never seduced by a novelty or gimmick. He was a constant in an ever-changing commercial world amongst the growth in importance of celebrity and media. As his career progressed, Penn tended to focus more on the inner subject using an unusually close vantage point. The result is a study of how far a person’s character can be shown on their face. Each portrait’s brilliance lies not in the subject’s celebrity but in their personality and disposition. The collection seems to prove the universality of human character, so much so that from walking around the exhibition the viewer would not feel their portrait to be out of place on the wall, so long as it was rendered through Penn’s impeccable and unfaltering lens.

Finalist number eighteen is Lauren Davies

On arrival at the exhibition I was overtaken by an eager visitor exclaiming to the attendant, 'This is my second visit to the exhibition it's simply wonderful!' Penn was said to have been able to capture the very essence of a person, I was even more intrigued to find out how the black and white, silvery grey framed prints running at eye level around the walls could incite such passion in admirers. What had Penn revealed through his camera lens?

He penned his subjects into a corner, photographed his sitters propped up awkwardly by chairs obscured by stiff rolls of carpet, choosing to capture people in the stark light of day, refusing to clean the floor of his studio for celebrities.

Known for his images of the Vogue fashion community the display seems at first at odds with the vibrancy and colour palette of the industry that Penn prolifically documented. It's clear that Penn intended to delve deeper into his subjects own constitution exposing the person below the surface. What would he capture in them that they did not want others to see?

Penn has been described as a photographer recording fashion history from the 1940s, taking his last photo in 2007 aged 92. I came to the end of my fourth revolution of the photos believing that Penn had documented modern cultural history itself based on the sheer range and level of celebrity of the people he photographed. I concluded that Penn had an amazing eye for tones, textures, elegance versus humility and the human condition in a way that sets him aside as a true genius behind the lens.

Finalist number seventeen is Jerome Kiel

From the forties to the noughties, Irving Penn used his artistic skills, his vision and unconventional approach to take photographs of an astonishing variety of people from the realms of writing, music, art and drama.

The exhibition is stunning, with its useful texts and 110 portraits. Their lighting and backgrounds, often two canvas panels, had an impact on every sitter. Each photograph reveals nuances, tensions and body language, a realism not shown by Irving Penn’s contemporaries.

In 1948, for example, violinist Yehudi Menuhin has his back to one panel. His right hand is high on the opposite one; he owns the space and smiles slightly. His left hand casually holds his violin by its scroll. However, the diminutive Georgia O’Keefe looks enclosed and slightly petrified.

In dull clothes, painter Joan Miro, sits in an easy chair, slightly self-conscious. His daughter, Dolores, wearing a floral dress, sits to the right. Her arm leans over the back of the dining chair. She holds his hand, tilting her head towards her father, relaxed, almost meditative. Interestingly, he is against a black background, she against a white, perhaps a reflection of their two personalities; a barrier between them, which only she is breaking through.

In 1950, Richard Burton, sits forward, making his head seem larger. His left elbow is leaning on the table, but his right hand comes round to protect it. This gives the impression he is strong, defensive, forthright and unyielding. He looks relaxed, yet, with his gaze fixed in a steely, but mischievous, stare, he is still an enigma.

Penn’s legacy is still challenging and enables us to see the photographs as artistic compositions through the media of a camera, with some rare and difficult developing techniques and the talents of an extraordinary, creative man.

Finalist number sixteen is David Coop

Sixty three years of photographic portraits demonstrate Penn’s creative consistency and technical mastery.

Not simply the faces of some of the most significant figures of the twentieth century, Penn’s portraits are enduring images which engage the viewer. They reveal a range of attitudes and reactions. Few of the subjects are smiling, although Duke Ellington, and a relaxed Spencer Tracy are exceptions.

Other such as Georgia O’Keefe and the Duchess of Windsor seem tense and stiff. A dishevelled Jacob Epstein displaying a missing trouser button and stained work clothes contrasts with the impeccably groomed Grace Kelly and the studied flamboyance of Jean Cocteau.

Penn’s photography changes from full length portraits of subjects posed against plain backdrops through head and shoulder crops to intense facial studies. His images are never less than exhilarating. The simple lighting, isolating, emphasising or extinguishing subjects’ features is ever present.

His compositions are delightful. Resorting to his early studies in graphic design, Penn creates geometrical patterns with his subjects’ limbs. Dali, ever the showman sits with arms and legs spread. A youthful Peter Ustinov and Nicole Kidman contort their bodies into angular poses.

Penn’s lighting and composition may hint at tone and contrast but graphic effect is ultimately controlled by his printing. Dense black shapes contrast with paler facial features illustrated by his images of Pere Couturier and Marlene Dietrich both photographed in 1948.

Whilst most of the photographs here are gelatin silver there are a number of platinum palladium prints including ones of Jesse Norman, Anais Nin and Richard Avedon. Platinum palladian printing is a laborious, time consuming process requiring a meticulous and disciplined technique resulting in prints containing unsurpassed depth, luminosity and an extraordinary range of tones which are a worthy high point of this excellently curated exhibition.

Finalist number fifteen is Maisie Gliddon

Irving Penn’s largest exhibition of his works in 50 years: A beautiful fluid journey of portraiture from the 1940’s to 2007. A collection of some over a 100 portraits from Vogue editorials to subtle intimate photographs. Penn’s work provides the spectator with a completely new level of celebrity portraiture. Each beautifully cropped and constructed photograph portrays the subject in a state of sublime vulnerability; stripping away the traditional connotations and boundaries of what is revealed in a portrait. “Very often what lies behind the façade is rare and more wonderful than the subject knows or dares to believe.”

Penn’s photographs are effortlessly timeless and most importantly incredible to look at. The focus of each photograph is not only on the dramatic contrasts of the black and white film, but also the soft lighting and piercing eyes of the photographed subjects. The stunning silver gelatin prints offer a rare vintage incandescent quality, in which most photographers can only hope to achieve today. There are several notable differences between Penn’s concepts of fashion photography, in comparison to today’s. The 00’s fashion photography has become part of lucrative celebrity culture; rather than a striking visual language, providing us with fuel injected monotonous dazed and confused ‘snapshot’ images. What does this say about this generation of photographers, so caught up in our fast paced society revealing how we have lost the true serenity and care of the photograph?

Penn’s success lies in the importance of precision, the little details such as gentle natural lighting and meticulous planned composition. Penn is and will continue to be a giant in world of photographic portraiture.

Finalist number fourteen is Surabhi S Khanna

Don't just stand there, let's get to it/Strike a pose, there's nothing to it/Vogue!

Nothing to it? Sorry Madonna, but Irving Penn begs to differ: “very often what lies behind the façade is rare and more wonderful than the subject knows or dares to believe”.

The exhibition at the National Portrait Gallery displays an enchanting retrospective collection of vintage prints by the master of photographic portraiture himself. We are greeted by an amusing photograph of one of Penn’s idols in 1944. A laurelled Giorgio de Chirico, gazes casually out of his gelatine printed domain, (bench)marking the start of our journey through time, observing how Penn’s minimalistic style of photography to capture the true nature of an individual evolved.

Curator Magdalene Keaney has compiled a comprehensive, captivating display of works that show clear stylistic progression and Penn’s thoughts on photography through time. From full length shots with un-glamorous props such as the portrait of Georgia O’ Keeffe dwarfed by two large wooden boards at her sides to close-ups of the face focussed on the gestures expressing traits of the sitter that would not normally be apparent. The portrait of designer Issey Miyake, glowering out from beneath a dark hood is one of my favourites from the series, dramatic brooding and totally unconventional- much like his technology driven fashion line.

Every photograph competes for your attention despite the simple setting in a low lit gallery(- an homage to Penn’s religious use of tungsten lighting perhaps?). Penn’s clever use of lighting to produce the dramatic contrasts of light and shadow seen in portraits of: “Woody Allen as Charlie Chaplin” or of showgirl Josephine Baker, is in full view for all to admire at the exhibition.

These photographs are only making guest appearances at the National Portrait Gallery till the 6th of June, so why not experience the power of portrait photography through the lens of Irving Penn?

Finalist number thirteen is Lauren Rotenberg

Irving Penn Portraits at the National Portrait Gallery successfully traces the evolution of the American photographer’s studio portraits from 1947-2007, emphasizing the continuity and consistency of his vision, sensitivity to his subject, and technical finesse. The chronological presentation of 123 black and white photographs highlights Penn’s transition from full-length staged portraits to cropped and dramatic headshots of leading cultural figures. It celebrates his interest in the medium of photography (and its limits) as a way to capture the ‘interiority’ of sitters and to discover, as Penn states, “what lies behind the façade.”

Penn’s innovative use of simple grey backdrops, natural lighting and minimal props resists the narratives of complex settings and costumes. Claustrophobic corners draw the viewer into the intimate space of the sitter, and allow for subtle gestures, expressions, and individual uniqueness to emerge. Penn’s mastery of triangular compositions and sharp contrasts between light and dark (the chiaroscuro technique inspired by de Chirco) produce dramatic movement and tension within the frames. This play of light and shadow, revelation and concealment, is best articulated in portraits of Pablo Picasso, Issey Miyake, and Arthur Miller.

The modest mention of Penn’s travels in Europe, experimentation with platinum printing techniques, and commercial experience at Vogue results in an incomplete portrayal of his achievements. With the exception of four magazines secured in glass, Penn’s portraits are removed from their original context of fashion magazines that, accompanied by editorial texts, constructed popular notions of mid-20th century femininity. While Penn “challenged and changed the expectation of studio portraiture,” his relevance endures as a fashion photographer who conveyed a certain regal, chic, or coy femininity; here, represented only in the photographs of Grace Kelly, Audrey Hepburn, and Lisa Fonssagrives-Penn. Although intimate and illuminating, the exhibition falls short of producing the greatest portrait of a legendary photographer.

Finalist number twelve is Renske Mann

Judging by his portraits, Irving Penn could have excelled in any medium other than photography. His classic compositions are mathematically precise and symmetrical. He was the first to use plain backgrounds for minimal distraction. Cecil Beaton said Penn made life hard for himself, using few props and simple lighting. Remarkably, he remained an innovator throughout his 70-year photographic career.

Penn evidently felt the need to prove himself a painter’s equal. The Angel (1946) could pass for a Goya etching: the wrestler's grotesque, oversized head, chest and fists are bathed in diffused lighting. Balanced on his right foot, his body tilts towards the light source, giving a rare illusion of movement.

Another photograph of a group of ballet dancers resembles a monotone Degas pastel. A party of Vogue photographers is inspired by Manet’s Dejeuner Sur l’Herbe and the painterly influence persists into the ‘fifties: the hard-edged minimalism of Harlequin Dress (1950), modelled by his wife, Lisa Fonssagrives-Penn, is not a million miles from Mondrian.

Penn distilled character by exploring gesture, so hands and body language are often as important as faces. He photographed Truman Capote twice, once in the ‘forties and again in the ‘sixties. Both images reflect his style evolution through the decades. The first shows the author clearly unhappy, half-kneeling and crouching on a chair, like a Guantanamo stress position. Wrapped in a tweed coat, the pose is secretive, defensive, with hands tucked deep inside pockets. The ‘sixties print typifies Penn’s later cropped close-ups, showing a jaded Capote rubbing his eyes in fatigue, specs dangling from one hand.

Penn’s darkroom wizardry was legendary, but in the final analysis, paraphrasing Cezanne on Monet, “he was just an eye, but what an eye!” Paradoxically with his fashion background, he seemed best at photographing older men and women. It required no master of Penn’s stature to do justice to Audrey Hepburn or Grace Kelly. Their beauty spoke for itself.

Finalist number eleven is Lauren Price-Evans

Entering a room full of faces is hardly unanticipated in the National Portrait Gallery, but there is something unique about the faces captured by Irving Penn that captivates the viewer. The images are brutally sincere; it is more like stumbling into a room with windows into the private lives of the sitters.

Penn’s portraits, with their bare backgrounds and natural lighting, seem at first to distil their subjects. Standing in an empty studio, the subjects are extracted from their usual environments and forced to reveal themselves, removed from the rest of the world. One portrait of Georgia O’Keefe is exemplary of this, the artist known for her vividly colourful and boundary pushing paintings is in black and white, confined to a corner with arms behind her back. We are forced to contemplate the woman behind the paintings, looking at her stance and facial expression. Other photographs cut out most of the subject’s body entirely, severing off tops of heads, in order to concentrate on the exquisite details in the face. But the distillation process isn’t perfect; the ground is often littered with scraps of fabric or cigarette butts and when the focus is on the face, every flaw is vulnerably visible. Penn manages to isolate his subjects from the connotations surrounding them and expose their humanity.

However, the artist seems simultaneously aware of the fact that he will never fully portray the inner-face of his sitters. Penn says himself that ‘...a limitation of our medium is that the inside is recordable only in so far as it is apparent on the outside.’ His later works reflect this sentiment as they give equal attention to the detail we can see and that which we cannot. He reminds us to look beyond general outward appearances and focus in closer.

Finalist number ten is Josephine Thum, aged 14

I have always thought a photographer cannot be easily recognised. They cannot stylise their work in the varying and diverse ways that painters, or sculptors can. This exhibition proved me wrong.

Irving Penn’s sense of personal style was so strongly developed I felt I could sense him in each of his photographs. He has a way of capturing a person’s essence, and emotions so well, that I almost felt intrusive looking at them. Many of the portraits, such as Al Pacino’s, were extremely personal; defining every contour and detail of the face with striking lighting, but also bringing forward something deeper behind it. The exhibition showed 120 of his portraits, spanning Penn’s career, from 1944 – 2007. I found it fascinating to see how his work progressed, and although none of the portraits were wildly different, I found every one beautifully simplistic, Penn having engineered the shot to the height of minimalism.

Having been a fashion photographer for Vogue for many years, Penn’s portraits often have a stylised flair, and I thought this was reflected strongly in the exhibition. For those who like fashion, there were many of his portraits centred around it. The monochrome and simplicity of the portraits were very contemporary, and far ahead of his time, one of the things, I thought, that defined him as an artist. It was also interesting to see the collection of famous faces Penn shot; Christian Dior, Audrey Hepburn, Salvador Dali, Pablo Picasso and many more. If you are an art enthusiast, I would definitely recommend this innovative exhibition. I found his range of subjects fascinating, and the perceptive way in which they are presented quite stunning. I walked into this exhibition knowing little about Irving Penn, but I now regard him as one of the most talented photographers of the 20th century.

Finalist number nine is Carlo Chinca

Born in 1917, New Jersey, Irving Penn studied design under Alexey Brodovitch at the Philadelphia Museum School of Art. In 1938 he moved to New York and worked as a freelance designer, making drawings of shoes for Harpers Bazaar which earned him enough money to buy his first camera, a Rolleiflex. His work has ranged from street photographs of America in the 1940’s to fashion, celebrity portraits and still life advertising photographs for Harpers Bazaar and Vogue. He travelled the world with a portable studio photographing the indigenous people of Morocco, Papua New Guinea, Cameroon, Dahomey, Peru and more.

This exhibition is made up of 123 black and white portraits laid out in chronological order beginning in 1944 and ending in 2007 - two years before Penn’s death. The majority of these portraits were taken in his studio using simple backdrops of painted cloth, panels and carpet. The lighting is simple - often soft powdery light from a window or skylight.

Penn’s pictures are like Rembrandt’s paintings and charcoal drawings - deep black shadows giving way to bright highlights with all details revealed. Stark, sculptural, full of folds and textures. Figures seated, figures crammed into corners. Picasso peers from under his sombrero, Alfred Hitchcock hunched and mysterious, Marlene Dietrich black caped and radiant. Rudolf Nureyev with a quizzical stare, while in contrast, Grace Kelly strikes a typical pose and simply ‘glows’. A 1946 portrait ‘The Angel’ is a full length picture of professional wrestler Maurice Tillet striking a pugilistic pose with bare chest, black tights, gigantic head, like a grotesque Coney Island freak.

Irving Penn is photography’s nonpareil - a champion of chiaroscuro and master of silver gelatin and platinum printing techniques. He created a style most photographers find almost impossible not to imitate. In this digital age, it’s a breath of fresh air to view the prints of the past.

Finalist number eight is Harry Burke

The freshness of Irving Penn's photography is evident from his earliest portrait on display at this highly appealing exhibition, which captures one of the photographer's formative heros, the Italian artist Giorgio de Chirico, standing bemusedly at the bottom of the Spanish Steps, with a naturalism that is almost as spontaneous as the chance meeting itself. Yet despite never changing his intimate and personable approach to the photo portrait, this freshness is not lost throughout the fifty years of Penn's illustrious career, even to today's image weary eyes.

One of the main characteristics of Penn's work was his belief that portrait photography should reveal the true character behind the facade of the human face, and this is achieved in a manner that is as charming as it is honest, as embodied in the cigarette butts and studio debris that he doesn't care to omit. However his real secret was his ability to reveal as much of the artist as of the subject, which forms a large part of what makes this exhibition so revealing.

What is truly striking about this highly impressive body of work, though, is the range of incredible characters that one can meet, creating almost a role call of some of the most fascinating figures of the twentieth century. For what I find most compelling about Penn's work, from the full body portraits of his early years to the closer, cropped compositions of his later studies, is that fact that almost all of his subjects are looking into the camera, and as such at the viewer. Nowhere else is it possible to go eyeball to eyeball to such an illustrious and iconic range of personalities in one space, ranging from Duke Ellington through to Jean Cocteau and even an eccentrically posed Salvador Dali. This is the true magic of the exhibition.

Finalist number seven is Alex Van Baalen

In a frantic world of instant imagery and throw away fashion this exhibition of his work is like stepping through the looking glass into a world where time stands still and one learns to see the world through the lens of one of the great photographers of our time.

Penn once said, ‘A good photograph is one that communicates a fact, touches the heart and leaves the viewer a changed person’. This is true of this exhibition where his love of capturing both the beauty and rawness of his subjects leaves you feeling both drawn to the boldness of the images and yet also slightly unsettled by their stark reality.

It is in his fashion photography where this sense of timelessness is most felt. It is not so much about how the clothing is worn but more about creating dramatic shapes. Shapes that have a restrained air about them, yet also a boldness of presence. One is transported back to a time where a mere gesture with the hand or look from below a heavily painted eyelid could convey a sense of sophistication lost today. These images have inspired many of today’s great fashion designers to translate this feeling into a look. Clothes that reek of sophistication yet have an underlying raw sensuality about them. A reoccurring theme in all art forms and yet captured so simply by Penn making him as relevant today as he was at the height of his popularity. A modern hero reminding us all that less is still very much more.

Finalist number six is Rosalind Berwald

The 120 photographs in the show reveal Penn’s desire for the hidden truth, the soul beneath.

Penn, who died last year, was a man who painted with his camera. His idea of placing his subjects in cupboard-like corners, against grey tonal backgrounds, was unique.

Trapped in the corner like a naughty boy huddled in his overcoat, his face lit naturally by an overhead skylight, Jacques Fath holds an open pair of scissors, trying to carve his way out.

The light is exquisite, sheer craft: ballet dancers in their confinement, photographed in snowy drops of light, almost dissolve away. He never minded the mess on the studio floor - to Penn it is what happened with the camera that mattered.

Everyone famous beat a path to his door. Francis Bacon is pictured in black, eyes looking upwards, with every mark and spot on his face visible. Truman Capote, finger pressed into his cheek, forming dark hollows and deep ridges, appears like a bare vision of reality. Hitchcock, sitting on a roll of grubby carpet, clearly hides a surprised look on his face. How did he get up those six flights of stairs to Penn's studio? Can it be the same carpet Marlene Dietrich sits on, back towards us, bathed in light?

Before you leave look at the glorious portrait of Duke Ellington, his hand holding the collar of his brilliant white coat. This is Penn at his best; a revelation of Ellington's joyful soul.

Finalist number five is Ama Scott

Irving Penn once wrote that “very often what lies behind the façade is rare and more wonderful than the subject knows or dares to believe.”

A collection of works by the renowned photographer at London’s National Portrait Gallery this spring certainly encapsulates these words. Penn’s portraits are subtle, unassuming, and yet entirely provocative.

The exhibition displays a selection of the most celebrated characters of the last century, including Marlene Dietrich, Pablo Picasso and Truman Capote. In showing the sitters at their most personal, and in many cases vulnerable, Penn gives a moving portrayal not often associated with such icons.

The metamorphosis in style that takes place over Penn’s fifty-year career is especially evident, from his full-body captures of the Forties, to more intimate close-up portraits of later years. The finest points of his subjects’ personalities are unashamedly displayed by the photographer in surroundings powerful due to their sheer simplicity.

Perhaps the most memorable work of the exhibition is that of French novelist Colette, taken in 1951, three years before her death. One of few photographs taken at this stage in her life, the portrait is witty and charming, yet chilling.

Another highly original depiction is Penn’s 1988 portrait of fashion designer Issey Miyake. Only one side of the sitters face is revealed, the other being cloaked in fabric. What is so unsettling is Miyake’s single, penetrating eye, which mesmerizes the viewer, not unlike a snake with its prey.

Through his extraordinary control over light and shadow, Penn tactfully delves into the individual qualities of each sitter, whilst at the same time leaving them with their sense of mystery intact. It is such innovation that sets Irving Penn apart from his peers, making him one of the most unique and evocative photo-portraitists of his generation.

Finalist number four is Jane Whitfield

The endlessly unique human figure takes centre stage in this magnificent collection of portraits by Irving Penn: a dazzling company of cultural figures taken during a career that began in the early 1940s and went on to span seven decades. The poet W H Auden (captured here by Penn looking somewhat benign and unusually smooth-faced) wrote that the primary subject of art would always be for him ‘the human clay’: Penn’s black and white photographs give us this human clay with an intense clarity, simplicity and beauty.

The work is imbued with these qualities by Penn’s insistence that his sitters come before him in the equal space of his sparse studio: standing on a bare wooden floor, cornered by two unpainted stud walls the Duchess of Windsor is a wary, monochrome ‘geisha’; kneeling on a hard wooden chair Truman Capote is a fallen, unrepentant angel. Posed and composed, constrained and unconstrained, natural and unnatural light seeming to illuminate them from within, rarely is anything allowed to distract the subjects’ gaze from meeting the master’s lens. If props to identify the sitter are allowed they seem an almost unnecessary extravagance: Yehudi Menuhin’s violin, Cecil Beaton’s nude with a camera, Charles James’ tailor’s dummy and Francis Bacon’s scrappy copies of Rembrandt’s self-portraits.

Penn’s use of this equal space gives his work not just a sense of unity but of timelessness: one’s anchor to a period and a time may only be a sartorial one (only Balthus in a paint spattered coat tied with string looks suitably bohemian). ‘Pictures with people in’ was the deceptively simple title David Hockney gave his first solo show in 1963: ‘[Photographs] with people in ’ would be an equally appropriate title for curator Magdalene Keaney’s stunning Penn retrospective in 2010.

Finalist number three is David Graham

For those unfamiliar with Penn’s work, you might be struck on first impressions that you are seeing a slightly out of date series of black and white portraits that have little relevance in today’s age. Yes, the portraits are mainly of famous people, from Picasso to Nicole Kidman, but they tell us more about the subjects than most photographs that we see today. And that is Penn’s genius.

He turns the face into a topographic landscape where every pore, expression and texture is nuanced in a manner that most photographers appear incapable of today. His subjects are revealed warts and all without regard to their status or importance and fortunately for the viewer, before the days of publicists, stylists and Photoshop.

Penn originally trained as a graphic designer and his photographs are about shape and the design on the page. Many of the images are truly black and white not shades of grey and it is the extreme contrast between light and shade that further distinguishes Penn’s images.

The exhibition spans some seven decades of his work and one clearly sees a progression as he finds his confidence and defines his style. The sitters frequently look uncomfortable and that is because he meant them to. Only in the last decade of his life does his work lose its magic for me as he experiments more with modern trends in lighting and appears to have given in to the demands of editors and stylists to be nice to his subjects.

However, the exhibition is a must for anyone who aspires to take portraits or is interested to see famous figures of the past 60 years stripped bare of their pretensions.

Finalist number two is John Hooton

The exhibition of Irving Penn’s portraits at the National Portrait Gallery starts from 1944, with whole plate contact prints made directly from the negative without any enlargement. Penn began by using large format bellows cameras which are the perfect discipline to formulate technical finesse. Like his sitters, the photographer is detached from the camera, appraising with his eyes rather than through a viewfinder. Large format necessitated slow exposures. His subjects had to remain still and this is reflected in the watchful but fixed poses, some wedged between studio flats.

There is an artist’s roughness around the edges of his work. Backgrounds are dirty, the dull daylight from the studio roof is just adequate, and the expressions of his subjects are dispassionate. This is the essential Penn; his photographs are slightly off beat. He is not searching for the kind of perceived perfection that we are familiar with today, but strips the process to the bare fundamentals, extracting a raw depth from his sitters. He creates a timeless quality that is the key to lasting appeal.

In the mid fifties he began using roll film cameras. The new measured animation of his subjects is noticeable. He also tried tight cropping, cutting the tops of heads which was clearly an influence upon latter day photographers. The technical excellence of Penn’s work is still an enduring inspiration for photographers who strive for the perfect black and white print. Subtle changes throughout the tonal range; solid blacks that graduate to small white highlights.

His approach to photography was as sturdy as a wooden tripod. He was not seduced by the appeal of 35mm convenience, preferring the type of equipment where photography began.

His legacy to humanity as a photographic portrait authority has never been surpassed, both in merit and elegance.

Finalist number one is Jia Yun Cheng.

I stepped into the exhibition almost breathless, anticipating a gigantic wall-to-ceiling print of “pioneer supermodel” Lisa Fonssagrives shrouded in a hat of pheasant feathers. But the first photo in the Irving Penn Portraits exhibition was a small, absurd headshot of the artist Giorgio de Chirico, his head surrounded by bizarre olive branches. His indignant glare immediately extinguished my juvenile fantasy.

Penn’s early work of the 1940s is printed in modest size. His sitters are often clad in black, drawing your eye to the sitter’s exposed face. The pioneer of modern architecture, Le Corbusier, sat not perched, but languid, upon a grey hill of cloth. Penn’s inclination in his full-length portraits towards dark garment and simple prop, reminiscent of Velàzquez’s court paintings, delivers the universal form of the human body hidden yet accentuated: beautiful but quiet statements of immaculate composition. Colour is neutralized by Penn’s monochrome palette, the superficial trademark of this exhibition.

Superficial, because it is hardly Penn’s palette that defines his work. It is how he wields it to narrate a story that is about what we all like to think we conceal well. Alfred Hitchcock, photographed in 1947, gazed at me with idle menace like a rotund, if somewhat amused Mona Lisa. In Dali’s unconventionally symmetrical portrait, his sitting figure is an inviting ‘X’ that accentuates the madness patterned into his uneven gaze. As with all of Penn’s work, dramatic tonal contrast fights a tense battle with stillness.

The play between dynamism and austerity is the true trademark of Penn’s 60plus-year oeuvre, springing from Penn’s patient yet restless search for truth behind the enigma of the human face.

Fonssagrives fantasy forgotten, I left this exhibition educated about how beauty is not always about vogue, rather, one finds beauty in this invigorating tribute to Penn’s immortal portraits.
