Rep. Rush Holt Reintroduces Military Suicide Prevention Act, Hopes Senate Doesn't Block It For The Third Time

WASHINGTON -- As the National Guard and Army Reserve grapple with an increasing number of suicides amongst members, Rep. Rush Holt (D-N.J.) reintroduced legislation on Tuesday to help combat the trend, hoping that the Senate does not block it -- for the third time.

The major piece of Holt's bill would require the Defense Department to ensure that every member "of the Individual Ready Reserve, Individual Mobilization Augmentees, or the Inactive National Guard who have served at least one tour in either Iraq or Afghanistan will receive a counseling call from properly trained personnel not less than once every 90 days so long as the service member remains a member." If they were determined to be at risk, they would receive counseling or mental health treatment.

The legislation is modeled on a program run by the University of Medicine and Dentistry of New Jersey, which provides veterans with peer counseling and clinical assessments. Unlike the program proposed by Holt, however, veterans and their families are the ones to initiate assistance.

This is the third time that Holt has introduced his bill. On the previous two occasions, the legislation passed the House but didn't make it past the Senate. In December, the congressman told The Huffington Post that he believed Sen. John McCain (R-Ariz.) was the one who blocked it.

"He [McCain] said having these counselors check in with the Reservists every few months was overreaching," said Holt, relaying a phone conversation he had had with the senator. "I asked him in what sense it was overreaching. Surely he didn't think there wasn't a problem, did he? I must say I don't understand it."

A McCain spokesperson denied this version of events, pointing the finger at House negotiators.

"The need for this is only getting greater," said Holt in an interview with The Huffington Post explaining why he's hoping the third time will be a charm for his bill. "It's the second year in a row that more soldiers have died at their own hands than in combat in Afghanistan or Iraq. One of the things our bill would require is that the data [for suicides] be collected for the Individual Ready Reserve, which it is not now. But we think that the need in this specific area is greater than it ever has been."

When asked how he will try to get the Senate on board this time, Holt said he will be listening to his colleagues from New Jersey. "Senators have a way of talking amongst themselves, and I am going to take my cue from Sen. [Frank] Lautenberg and [Bob] Menendez on the best way to approach the members of the Senate Armed Services Committee.

Holt's legislation is named after Coleman S. Bean, a young sergeant who took his own life in 2008 after being deployed for a second tour of duty in Iraq despite being diagnosed with post-traumatic stress disorder. As a member of the IRR, he found fewer resources available to him than to veterans and active-duty members. Coleman's mother, Linda Bean, was on Capitol Hill with Holt on Tuesday for the introduction of the bill.

Twenty-five soldiers -- both active duty and those on inactive status -- died by suicide each month in 2010, according to Army figures. There were 101 confirmed or suspected suicides among National Guard soldiers on inactive status, more than double the 48 in 2009.

Holt said the Pentagon needs to be doing more. When asked what sort of response he has received from military officials about his bill, he said that he usually receives two comments.

"One is we really need to do this, and the other is we're already doing," he said. "When you press them on what they're already doing -- no, they're not already doing this. They have some crisis intervention programs. It's true that both the high command and the mid-level officers are more attuned to suicide prevention and mental health than they ever have been, but that's not to say they are well-attuned to it."

The Pentagon did not return a request for comment, but Army Chief of Staff Gen. Peter Chiarelli and others have acknowledged the particular challenges facing members of the Guard and Reserve. He recently said the military must be even more aggressive in its efforts to alleviate the economic stress that soldiers often face and can contribute to troubles.

"There are no universal solutions to address the complexities of personal, social, and behavioral health issues that lead to suicide within the Army," said Col. Chris Philbrick, deputy director of the Army's health-promotion and risk-reduction task force, who has been working to reduce the suicide rate.
