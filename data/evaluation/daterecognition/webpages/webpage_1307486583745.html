    • ID theft costs £2bn a year

    Chris Warmoll, This is Money
    8 March 2005
    Vote

    IDENTITY fraud is costing British consumers and businesses more than £2bn a year. Security experts warn the trend is likely to get worse and urge consumers to take immediate steps to protect their details from slipping into criminal hands.

    The huge rise in identity theft, which has grown eightfold in the past five years, has been partly fuelled by the success of chip and Pin card technology, as criminals seek new ways to obtain personal details to gain goods and money illegally.

    Neil Munroe, external affairs director of Equifax, said fraudsters can simply rifle through bins for credit card slips, utility bills and other personal information.

    'If fraudsters can't use a stolen credit card they may turn to impersonating innocent victims. And, when it can take up to 300 hours to clear a victim's name, we should be concentrating on prevention rather than cure.

    'The scary thing is you don't know if your ID has been been cloned. You should get a print out of your credit file, which will show if there has been an application for credit or a recent agreement.

    'Fraudsters need to get three or four pieces of information which contain enough data to open new accounts and take out fraudulent credit agreements and loans in your name. With publicly available records like the electoral roll and Land Registry, criminals can find out all they need to know.'

    The Government�s love affair with ID cards, won't eradicate the problem but should prove a useful tool, he added.

    'Consumers may be shocked to learn that criminals can legally send off for other people's birth certificate and driving-licence. With a birth certificate, criminals will know a mother's maiden name, and can then apply for a passport.'

    Sandra Quinn, of the Association for Payment Clearing Services (Apacs), said without chip and Pin technology, card fraud alone would have hit £800m in 2004.

    'This is not just a bank issue. The onus is on individuals to secure all their personal details. If regular statements do not arrive it could indicate that it has been intercepted. The Home Office is aware of many of the loopholes being used by ID fraudsters and is tackling them head on.'

    Top Tips to keep your details safe:

    • Check your credit report frequently. This is the most effective way of identifying fraudsters using your ID. See our guide to checking and repairing your credit rating.

    • Shred statements, bills and direct mail. These contain vital personal information.

    • When you move home redirect your mail to your new address by contacting the Post Office.

    • When you receive your new Pin for your card do not write it down and keep it anywhere near your card. If you change your Pin or password, make sure they are all different. Also, don't use your birthday, phone number or other obvious passwords. Never reveal your passwords or Pin to anyone.
