Finance ministers from the United States, China and other Asia-Pacific powers have rejected an early end to the huge stimulus packages they implemented to fight the global economic crisis.

"We agreed that the pace of implementing exit strategies should take into account different stages in the economic recovery of member economies, the type of policy measures to be phased out, and any spillovers caused by our strategies," they said on Thursday.

The Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation (APEC) forum ministers issued a statement after an annual meeting in Singapore, ahead of a weekend summit of their leaders including US President Barack Obama.

Meanwhile, US Treasury Secretary Timothy Geither said it was important to have a strong US dollar and praised China's role in the global economic recovery.

"It is very important to the United States that we have a strong dollar, that we continue to focus on improving our fundamentals, that we sustain confidence in not just the stability of our financial system but in our capacity as a country, as growth recovers, to move our fiscal position back in the balance," he told a news conference in Singapore.

"That's going to be very important to recovery in the United States," he said after the finance ministers' meeting.

Geithner acknowledged that given the importance of the US economy and its currency, "we bear a special responsibility for being a source of stability and strength for the global economy".

He added that China was playing a "major role" in powering the world back to growth, side-stepping questions over whether Beijing is artificially weakening its currency to boost Chinese exports.

Asia-Pacific finance ministers including from China and the United States pledged to pursue "market-oriented exchange rates", following criticism over the Chinese yuan's fixed rate.

"We will undertake monetary policies consistent with price stability in the context of market-oriented exchange rates that reflect underlying economic fundamentals," ministers said in a statement.
