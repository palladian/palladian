The Inevitable March of Recorded Music Towards Free
  • Tweet
  • View Comments
Michael Arrington
Oct 4, 2007

2007 is turning out to be a terrible year for the music industry. Or rather, a terrible year for the the music labels.

The DRM walls are crumbling. Music CD sales continue to plummet rather alarmingly. Artists like Prince and Nine Inch Nails are flouting their labels and either giving music away or telling their fans to steal it. Another blow earlier this week: Radiohead, which is no longer controlled by their label, Capitol Records, put their new digital album on sale on the Internet for whatever price people want to pay for it.

The economics of recorded music are fairly simple. Marginal production costs are zero: Like software, it doesn’t cost anything to produce another digital copy that is just as good as the original as soon as the first copy exists, and anyone can create those copies (meaning there is perfect competition and zero barriers to entry). Unless effective legal (copyright), technical (DRM) or other artificial impediments to production can be created, simple economic theory dictates that the price of music, like its marginal cost, must also fall to zero as more “competitors” (in this case, listeners who copy) enter the market. The evidence is unmistakable already. In April 2007 the benchmark price for a DRM-free song was $1.29. Today it is $0.89, a drop of 31% in just six months.

P2P networks just exacerbate the problem (or opportunity) further, giving people a way to speed up the process of creating free copies almost to the point of being ridiculous. Today, a billion or so songs are downloaded monthly via BitTorrent, mostly illegally.

Eventually, unless governments are willing to take drastic measures to protect the industry (such as a mandatory music tax), economic theory will win out and the price of music will fall towards zero.

When the industry finally capitulates and realizes that they can no longer charge a meaningful amount of money for digital recorded music, a lot of good things can happen.

First, other revenue sources can and will be exploited, particularly live music, merchandise and limited edition physical copies of music. The signs are already there – the live music industry is booming this year, and Radiohead is releasing a special edition box set of their new album for Â£40.00 simultaneous to the release of their “free” digital album.

Second, artists and labels will stop thinking of digital music as a source of revenue and start thinking about it as a way to market their real products. Users will be encouraged (even paid, as radio stations are today) to download, listen to and share music. Passionate users who download music from the Internet and share it with others will become the most important customers, not targets for ridiculous lawsuits.

The price of music will likely not fall in the near term to absolutely zero. Charging any price at all requires the use of credit cards and their minimum fees of $0.20 or more per transaction, for example. And services like iTunes and Amazon can continue to charge something for quality of service. With P2P networks you don’t really know what you are getting until you download it. It could, for example, be a virus. Or a poor quality copy. Many users will be willing to pay to avoid those hassles. But as long as BitTorrent exists, or simple music search engines like Skreemr allow users to find and download virtually any song in seconds, they won’t be able to charge much.

Update: There are some blog responses to this post that are, inevitably, complaining about fairness. Arguing against basic economics makes about as much sense as arguing against gravity. Zero marginal cost + competition (anyone can create a copy of a song) results in a zero price, unless government creates artificial barriers to a free market.

Update 2: NBC Universal chief Jeff Zucker: “We need, across the board, to move IP enforcement up the agenda of the federal government.” Scary stuff.

Update 3: Paul Glazowski writes a rebuttal, essentially arguing from the fairness perspective. I talk about the fairness argument in the comments below a lot. As I say below, gravity may not be fair, either, but its inevitable. Paul also says the medium has “value,” which I don’t dispute. But the fact is that zero marginal production cost plus perfect competition (every consumer is also a potential producer of any song) inevitably equals a zero price. I think Paul’s main point is, even though he doesn’t say it, that government should step in and set a “fair” price of $5 – $8 per album. Of course, that will involve getting the feds involved to enforce these laws (see update 2 above), or some kind of music tax (which creates really, really bad incentives).

    While true that the “economics of recorded music are fairly simple,” the business model that the music industry has followed for decades isn’t.

    The music industry is built entirely around the selling of recorded music in one format or another. Merchandise is sold to offset costs of touring. Touring offsets costs of videos and other promotional efforts. If those efforts are successful, and if the music sells, the first thing those sales offset are the recording costs. Once those are offset, along with all other “administrative” costs such as representation fees or distribution costs, the artists start making money. In other words, the model dictates that there are no net profits from any revenue stream other than the actual and eventual selling of the music.

  • Paul

    A few things, as I skim through the posts:

    1) Most people clearly have a misunderstanding as to the purpose of copyrights in the US. Their original intent was to foster and encourage competition. They were set at fairly short times (which, IMHO, have been wrongly extended). A copyright was to protect a work for a short amount of time, allowing the content creator to profit from their creation, then it would expire–with the intent that NEW content would be created. The intent of copyright was not to protect the work (and provide profit) indefinitely. (This same principle applies to patents, too)

    2) Fairness is a red herring. There is no right to fairness…no expectation of fairness. Nothing is fair. It’s not “fair” that I can’t sing well enough to start up a band and be successful (not that being able to sing is a requirement for a successful band). The idea that the government ought to “make it fair” is based on emotionalism, not logic or reason. The government has an obligation to enforce property rights…that’s it.

    3) We’re just now, as a society, beginning to discuss what are the property rights associated with “intellectual” property (that is, non-physical property). When copyright law was written, barriers to entry were somewhat higher, but not as ubiquitous as they are now. As stated in the post, it costs virtually nothing for me to obtain the “intellectual” property of someone else (a recording of a performance, the text of a book, the code to run a blog). We have some people who argue that if its “out there” then it can be had for free (as in beer), like tuning a radio to a station. Others argue that control of it, regardless of it being “out there” remains with the property holder. But how does that work? I own a CD with recording on it. It’s my CD. I can destroy it, give it away, sell it, whatever. But what about the recording? Do I own it? I own a COPY of the recording, as does anybody else who owns the same CD. I didn’t sign a EULA stating that I’ve only got a license to listen to the recording. I bought a copy of the recording. Current copyright law says I may not copy the recording (for profit or not is a different, but related argument) until the copyright expires.

    4) The real argument I think most people have is with the recording companies, not the artists. Most people cannot understand why a CD costs more today than it did 20 years ago (in actual, not real dollars). Then, they’re even more boggled why a downloaded song costs the same. Most people have learned over the course of this debate that the artists get precious little for their efforts (with some exceptions). People cheer on artists who cut their ties with the industry and strike it out on their own. I suspect that these artists will continue to be successful, and even more so.

    5) Without condoning it, there will always be piracy. There are a relatively small group of people who feel they have the right to copy whatever is available. They are wrong in this… but they will not be eliminated.

    6) If I was a recording artist, my business model would be to give away content on the internet and then make it up in sales–much like Radiohead. I think they’ve probably got the best model. If my music is worth listenting to, worth wanting, then I can make it. If people don’t like it, it won’t matter what the business model is, I won’t be successful.

  • David S.

    First time poster here.

    I believe Michael is simply making the point that, with the incredible expansion and increased saturation of information technologies, means of distribution, etc., it will eventually be impossible, truly IMPOSSIBLE, to charge for music distribution. It’s over. It won’t happen anymore. It’s not about morality, or even about business models. It’s about the fact that our world is changing into one that is so permeated with the free exchange of information (which is only going to get easier with time) that the old business model won’t work anymore.

    That said, there IS a case being made (which I bet Michael would agree with) that there is, in fact, value to intellectual properties. I don’t think any reasonable person would dispute that. This whole issue of intellectual property rights and distribution has been bothering me for a long, long time, but after brainstorming about it, I think I’ve come up with an idea that might work. Let me know what you think.

    The old model was this: music produced had value. That music was paid for by the profits made from distributing the music. But in reality, the payment was entirely intended for the actual PRODUCTION of the music (not in a production cost sense, but in the fact that artists are making the music.) So money was essentially given to artists (actually, mostly the labels, but I digress) for their artistic ability and the release of new material.

    A couple of baseline facts that we can all agree on:
    1. Intellectual property does have value.
    2. Artists should be paid for what they produce.
    3. The digital age has made the distribution model, as it stands, obsolete.

    Any new model that is created then, logically, has to contain a method for paying artists for the production of new material, but with the free exchange of information, CANNOT be based on distribution. So how can we do that without it being based on distribution?

    (I mention labels here, but this method could be done without a middleman at all.)

    Simple: pre-orders. Big artists simply won’t release their album until there has been enough demand for it through payments for pre-orders. One could even take it a step further and have us pay artists for even RECORDING the music. Once it is made, then it is distributed for free.

    This would work for big artists, but what about up-and-coming ones? Well, that’s also simple. They gather exposure the old way: word of mouth, live tours, releasing music (for free) that gets popular. If they can’t spread by those traditional means, then they wouldn’t have made it in the old model either. Once they have built a fanbase willing to pay to hear them, they sign a record deal. Targets are set for how many they expect to sell for pre-orders, and once they are met, the album is released. People who pre-ordered can also get some sort of extra incentive for that act: a special edition album, early access, whatever.

    This method would even work for small artists. They could just set low targets with small labels. That way, they could get increased exposure, get paid proportionate to their fanbase, and still have incentive to make music.

    The only problem I see with this model is, of course, the obvious one of what if the band doesn’t reach their target? Well even in that regard, there are ways of making it work. If they reach, say, 70% of their target, they can release 70% of the album perhaps. Or, if they are let go because of disappointing sales, people simply get their money back (or credit for pre-ordering another album.) You could also work it to a certain amount of sales results in a song being released: say, every 30,000 sales equals one song.

    I could go into all sorts of details about it, but I think you get the idea. It’s definitely alien, and obviously the costs would have to be lower (people won’t drop 18 dollars for music they’ll get in the future, but they may be willing to drop 5 into the artist’s hat to keep them playing.)

    We have only ever been paying for the PRODUCTION of music in the first place. And now that distribution doesn’t work as a payment method, the only other option is to pay for the production itself. If anyone else has any ideas about a different model that would work, I’d love to hear it.
