Top 10 notebooks of February

By Darius Chang
Feb 28, 2006

New entrants to the list this month are the Toshiba Satellite L20, Acer Aspire 5500 series, Toshiba Qosmio G20, Fujitsu LifeBook S7020 and Samsung Q30 notebooks. They took over from the incumbent Sony VAIO VGN-TX17GP, Acer Ferrari 4000, Dell Inspiron 710m, MacBook Pro and Compaq Presario B2800 laptops.

Toshiba has both ends of the pricing spectrum represented with its budget Satellite L20 and flagship Qosmio G20 this month. Priced around S$2,000 (US$1,472.50), depending on the configuration, the poor performance and battery life of the former seem to matter less than its excellent keyboard and display. The Qosmio G20, on the other hand, costs nearly S$5,000 (US$3,681.26), but had so many impressive multimedia features that it received our coveted Editors' Choice award.

The Acer Aspire 5500 series has been superceded by the dual-core Acer Aspire 5600 series but retains its popularity due to the fact that the latter costs about S$800 (US$589) more. Retailing at S$1,898 (US$1,397.41), according to the latest notebook price watch, the excellent gaming performance of the Aspire 5500 series is tempered by its poor battery life.

The Fujitsu LifeBook S7020 and Samsung Q30 place a premium on portability. The LifeBook has strong security features in the form of a fingerprint sensor and Trust Platform Module (TPM) chip which makes it harder for hackers to elicit passwords. The Q30 forgoes an optical drive for a lighter and more portable package. Combined with a red color scheme, it provides a powerful persuasion for the fairer sex to buy this premium notebook.

1.  ASUS A6J (Core Duo T2500 Processor 2.0GHz, 1GB RAM)
 
CNET Asia rating: 7.3 out of 10
The good:  Powerful graphics performance; tough carbon fiber exterior; plays music discs without bootup; integrated Webcam; DVI port; wide array of connectivity options.
The bad:  Thick and heavy; poor speaker position; no easy access USB ports; no ExpressCard slot; stiff touchpad buttons; no security features for a business notebook; poor battery life.
The bottom line: The powerful performance, as well as the wealth of connectivity features, make this notebook suitable for almost any computing work. However, the lack of security features and ExpressCard slot are glaring omissions that mar an otherwise impressive business machine.

2.  Toshiba Satellite L20-C430 (Celeron M processor 380 1.6GHz, 256MB RAM)
 
CNET Asia rating: 4.9 out of 10
The good:  Low price; excellent keyboard; bright display.
The bad:  Mediocre performance; very poor battery life; no external volume controls.
The bottom line: The no-frills Satellite L20 offers the bare minimum for budget-conscious home users, but even at such a low price there are better options.

Review | See full specs | Rate this
3.  Dell Inspiron 630m (Pentium M Processor 750 1.86GHz, 512MB RAM)
 
CNET Asia rating: 7.3 out of 10
The good:  Ample multimedia control buttons; bright widescreen display; power indicator on the battery; good speaker position.
The bad:  Heavy and bulky; no pre-boot DVD play; integrated graphics chipset.
The bottom line: The cheapest configuration is adequate only as a basic computing machine. Fortunately, Dell offers upgrades, at a price, to power users looking for a multimedia notebook.

Review | See full specs | Rate this
4.  Fujitsu LifeBook P7120 (Pentium M Processor ULV 753 1.2GHz, 1GB RAM)       
 
CNET Asia rating: 8.5 out of 10
The good:  Very portable; long battery life; economy mode conserves battery; good speaker position; wide range of display brightness; strong security features; fanless design.
The bad:  No ExpressCard slot; integrated graphics chipset; no Gigabit ethernet.
The bottom line: For users unwilling to sacrifice features and yet demand a diminutive notebook, the Fujitsu LifeBook P7120 fits the bill perfectly.

Review | See full specs | Rate this
5.  Compaq Presario B1800 (Pentium M Processor 760 2.0GHz, 512MB RAM)
 
CNET Asia rating: 6.7 out of 10
The good:  Classy exterior; slim profile with an integrated optical drive; bright, widescreen display; reasonable price.
The bad:  The screen looks small due to a thick border; poor position of the easy-access USB port and audio jacks; integrated optical drive cannot be upgraded.
The bottom line: This notebook is suitable for students or executives in need of a serious work machine that doesn't look like it came out of a mass production facility.
