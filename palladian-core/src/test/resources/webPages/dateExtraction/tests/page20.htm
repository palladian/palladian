In Alchemists' Notes, Clues to Modern Chemistry

Published: April 10, 1990
(Page 2 of 5)
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print
  • Single-Page

The vast literature left by alchemists contains hundreds of such pictures, whose meanings are yielding to modern research. The alchemist lacked understanding of such modern chemical ideas as the nature of atoms; even Isaac Newton, who devoted much of his career to alchemy, listed the components of matter in traditional alchemical terms: earth, air, fire and water. Alchemy's bad name among modern scientists stems from its traditional association with astrology, mysticism and black magic, a tradition some 2,000 years old. Modern scientists see little or no merit in alchemy as science, regarding it as mostly naive superstition. The alchemists themselves often besmirched the reputation of their calling. Many were tricksters, and when their frauds and counterfeits were exposed they were sometimes put to death.

Nevertheless, the ranks of the alchemists included a few of the greatest scientists of all time, Isaac Newton and Robert Boyle among them. Although Newton's renown rests on his landmark discoveries in physics and mathematics, about half of his career was devoted to alchemy, a fact that has disturbed some of his modern admirers. But Dr. Betty Jo Teeter Dobbs, a professor of history at Northwestern University who has studied Newton's work for many years, regards alchemy as central to his career, not just an aberration.

''Newton's underlying object,'' she said, ''was not simply to learn about nature but to discern the activity of God in all of nature. Alchemists regarded everything in nature, minerals and planets included, as animated.'' Dr. Dobbs argues in a new book, ''Alchemical Death and Resurrection Newton: the Significance of Alchemy in the Age of Newton'' published by the Smithsonian Institution, that Newton, a puritan, feared what he considered to be the rise of atheism. He embraced the alchemical notion of universal animation, an infusion by God of the divine spirit into all things.

Dr. Mary Ellen Bowden of the Beckman Center for the History of Chemistry at the University of Pennsylvania said that only a handful of working chemists have ever attempted to reconstruct alchemical recipes in terms of modern chemistry. Among the most productive of these experimenters, she said, is Dr. Principe of Johns Hopkins.
